Beneficial Occupation Explained: What Landlords Need to Know

By: VacatAd Team Published: 22 May 2025 Read time: 10 min

For commercial asset-owners dealing with void properties, the phrase "beneficial occupation" may sound legalistic — but its meaning has very real financial consequences. Understanding what counts as occupation, what resets relief periods, and how to document compliant use is key. This month we break down the concept, recent case-law and how solutions such as those from VacatAd meet the test in a robust way.

What is beneficial occupation?

In rating law, for a property to be subject to business rates (i.e., occupied for rating purposes), the occupier must not only physically be in the property but meet several criteria:

  • actual occupation;
  • exclusive possession or control;
  • the occupation must provide some value or benefit to the occupier; and
  • it must not be too transient.

In the landmark case R. (on the application of Principled Offsite Logistics Ltd) v Trafford Council (POLL) the court considered whether storage of goods purely to trigger occupation qualified. The judge held that whilst the goods storage had some purpose, the arrangement needed to reflect a genuine use rather than purely rates-avoidance.

Why this matters for void properties and rates relief

When a property becomes vacant, there is typically a relief period (three months for most properties, six months for some industrial units) during which business rates are not payable. However, for the relief period to reset — i.e., to start afresh after a short occupation and become possible again — the subsequent occupation must satisfy beneficial occupation criteria.

Critical Point: Many landlords assume that any minimal use will suffice — but mere token use may not stand up if challenged. For example, one industry article noted that "token leasing of floors to reset relief may be challenged."

What counts as a compliant occupation model?

A robust occupation model for void units should meet the following practical criteria:

  1. Genuine use of the space – Not just placing any items, but activity with purpose (e.g., community usage, connectivity-services).
  2. Duration – The occupation must be long enough to show control and benefit; extremely short leases (e.g., a few days) are unlikely to meet the threshold.
  3. Documentation – Lease or licence agreements, evidence of use, installation of service (such as Wi-Fi) help support the case.
  4. Value/benefit – The occupier should derive some benefit (e.g., running services, providing connectivity) and the landlord should not simply be renting a shell for tax reasons only.

By contrast, a purely artificial occupation intended solely to claim a new relief window risks challenge.

Practical checklist for landlords

  1. Review lease/licence duration – Aim for at least three months of real occupation to restore relief eligibility.
  2. Check the nature of occupation – Ask: what activity is done? Does it provide a service or value beyond simply being present?
  3. Document thoroughly – Keep records of connectivity installation, user logs, advertising activity or other operational metrics.
  4. Seek specialist advice – Given that local authorities and rating tribunals may increasingly challenge mitigation strategies, legal and tax-rating advice is prudent.
  5. Plan ahead – Understand when relief will end, when occupation is needed, what documentation you have, and how the property will be used in the interim.

Common pitfalls and how to avoid them

Short-term leases without substance

A one-week licence may not satisfy the "not too transient" test. Ensure occupation periods are substantial enough to demonstrate genuine use.

No real activity

If the property is physically occupied but no meaningful use is demonstrated, the authority may treat it as empty. Installation of active services like Wi-Fi demonstrates genuine activity.

Poor documentation

Without clear leases or evidence of benefit, defending occupation will be harder. Maintain comprehensive records of all occupation activities.

Assuming reset is automatic

The 13-week reset rule means that you must plan the 13-week occupation proactively. Simply having a short-term occupier won't automatically trigger a new relief period.

Case Study Example

In the POLL case, the court scrutinised whether storage arrangements constituted genuine beneficial occupation or were merely designed to avoid rates. The key takeaway: the purpose and substance of the occupation matters, not just the technical presence of goods or people in the property.

The legal framework in practice

Recent developments in rating law have made it clear that local authorities are taking a more robust approach to examining occupation claims. Several key principles have emerged:

  • Substance over form – The actual use and purpose matter more than the contractual arrangement alone.
  • Economic reality – Is there genuine economic benefit or commercial purpose to the occupation?
  • Public policy – Courts recognise the legitimate interest in preventing purely artificial arrangements designed solely to avoid tax.
  • Evidence requirements – Comprehensive documentation of actual use, not just contractual entitlement, is increasingly important.

Looking ahead: The evolving landscape

As local authorities face continued pressure on their budgets, scrutiny of business rates mitigation strategies is likely to increase. Property owners should expect:

  • More frequent challenges to occupation claims
  • Greater emphasis on evidence and documentation
  • Potentially shorter relief periods in future reforms
  • Increased penalties for non-compliant schemes

In this environment, having a demonstrably compliant, well-documented occupation strategy is not just good practice — it's essential protection against financial and legal risk.
