Securing Your Vacant Properties: How Vacatad Transforms Risks into Rewards in a Post-Pandemic World

By VacatAd Team • Aug 12, 2024 • 6 min read
In the aftermath of the COVID-19 pandemic, the landscape of Britain's town centres has been dramatically altered. Once-bustling high streets are now shadowed by increasing vacancy rates, with commercial properties left empty as businesses either pivot to remote working or succumb to the economic pressures of the past few years. Unfortunately, these vacant spaces have become targets for criminal activities, notably the industrial-scale farming of cannabis by organised crime groups.

Recent reports from law enforcement agencies paint a concerning picture: criminal gangs are exploiting abandoned shops, offices, and even former pubs and bingo halls to establish vast cannabis farms. With footfall in town centres significantly reduced, especially in the evenings, these illicit operations often go unnoticed until it’s too late. This troubling trend is symptomatic of the broader economic and social shifts brought about by the pandemic, highlighting the urgent need for innovative solutions to protect vacant properties (BBC News).

The Growing Threat to Vacant Properties

As commercial properties lie empty, they are increasingly at risk of being repurposed for illegal activities. In Newport, South Wales, for example, criminals used several floors of an abandoned department store to grow more than 3,000 cannabis plants. The street value of these drugs was estimated at £2 million, underscoring the scale of the problem. This isn’t an isolated incident; similar cases have been reported across the UK, from Scotland to Wales, revealing a nationwide issue that property owners cannot afford to ignore (The Telegraph).

The underlying causes of this phenomenon are multifaceted. The decline in high street activity, driven by a combination of increased home-working and reduced consumer spending, has left many commercial properties without tenants. This lack of occupancy creates a vacuum that criminal enterprises are all too eager to fill. Furthermore, with fewer people frequenting these areas, the usual signs of illegal activity—such as taped-up windows or bright lights during the night—are less likely to be noticed, allowing these operations to flourish (BBC News).
